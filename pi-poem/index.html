Pi poem

Written on Pi Day — 3/14/15. The words speak the first eighteen digits of π; a compass lays them onto a circle, and the circle paints every picture in the poem.

Three

One Four

One Five Nine

She

devours

those numbers´ line.

Why is that? - the question stands;

maybe ´cause it never ends,

like kids stories.

No end - no worries,

about what will come when all is done;

Two Six Five Three Five Eight Nine.

It isn´t the end - so is it the start?

Is it the thing u take apart

when u want to get the number?

Like love of trees through love of lumber?

Is it a circle, an oval, or wheels,

that power her strong number feels?

A disk, a vortex, a nice round tire,

that makes this girl a numberphile?

The thing that brings this girl to think

might be just a bling - a ring;

but the urge can´t be to be a wife

no rhyme for that - nowhere soon another Five

The urge is to go to places

she doesn´t care if decimal

all is well while her face is

full of big, but mainly round smile.

And her spirit free.

Seven Nine Free!

Two Free!

Colophon

Pi poem was written on the Pi Day of the century — 3/14/15, the date that spells the first five digits. The girl in the poem doesn´t read numbers: she devours them.

Its words — Three, One Four, One Five Nine… — hide the first eighteen digits: 3.14159 26535 89793 23. A compass lays them onto the circumference, the very curve that π measures. And the poem keeps painting that one circle. When it asks what you take apart to get the number, the page splits the circle into its diameter — c = π · d. Then come the tree rings of lumber, a steering wheel, a brake disk, a vortex of exhaust, a round tire, and a ring with a diamond that fades the moment marriage is mistaken for the urge.

When the circle unrolls into a straight line under her running feet, that line is the oldest illustration of π there is: a circumference is exactly π diameters long. Then it rolls back up — into a big, but mainly round smile.

The ending is a pun: Free sounds like Three. Seven Nine Free! Two Free! — her spirit slips loose and the poem closes on a 3, the digit it opened with. The circle, though, never closes. π carries on: 8 4 6 2 6 4 3 3 8 3…

And nowhere soon another Five is literally true: after the five in 26535 — the tenth digit — the next five appears only at the 31st decimal place. π would not offer a rhyme for wife for another 21 digits.
